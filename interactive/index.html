What they said before being executed.

By @mariepastora - Best on Chrome and desktop

In the United States, capital punishment is legal in 31 states. In 1976, after a four-year ban, the Supreme Court issued a ruling re-instating the death penalty in Gregg v Georgia. 1,479 inmates have been executed since then.




Texas has carried out most of these executions, putting to death 553 inmates. The state has the most active execution chamber in the country, and 225 convicts currently sit on its death rows.

Before they are executed, inmates can make a last statement. Some speak, others write and many decline, but we managed to retrieve 939 last statements, either in their entirety, or pieces of them.

Accross all states, years, races and genders, 397 inmates mentionned love. 294 mentionned a family, either theirs or the one of the person they killed.

These are the 16 female inmates that have been executed since 1976. Half of them left a last statement, either spoken or written.

And these statements mostly mentionned love.

Teresa Bean Lewis was convicted in 2002 for the murders of her husband, and of her stepson. She was executed in Virginia, in September 2010. Her final words went to Kathy, the daughter of the husband she killed:

"I just want Kathy to know that I love her, and I'm very sorry."

Lisa Ann Coleman was sentenced to death for the murder of her partner's son in July 2004. She was executed in Texas, in 2006:

"I just want to tell my family I love them; my son, I love him. The girls on the row, I love them and keep their heads up. Tell Darlie I love her, hand in hand. God bless y'all. I'm alright. Tell them I finished strong. I love y'all. I'm done. I love you, Richie. I love you. Thank you, Brad and John, all of y'all. God is good. I love you, Auntie. I'm done."

And four mentionned God.

Kimberley Lagayle McCarthy was convicted for the 1997 murder of her neighbor. She was executed in 2013, in Texas.

"This is not a loss. This is a win. You know where I'm going. I'm going home to be with Jesus. Keep the faith. I love you all. God is great."





Now let's talk about race.

506 African American inmates have been executed since 1976. This represents 34% of all the executions. However, non-Hispanic Blacks represent 13.7% of the country's total population, according to the Census Bureau's 2017 estimates.

In other words, Black defendants are more likely to receive the death penalty than White defendants. This is especially true if the victim of the Black defendant is white.

We could retrieve the final statements of 316 of them.

149 mentionned love. 108 mentionned family. 59 mentionned God. And 45 pronounced the word "Peace".

Let's see what it represents.





Here are all the 506 African American inmates who were executed.

A lot of them talked about love.

"This is justified. I love you. I'll see you again."

- Anthony Rozelle Banks, executed in Oklahoma, in September 2013 a murder committed in 1978.

"I send love to my family and friends and everyone who knows me."

- Johnile L. Dubois, executed in Virginia in 1998, for the 1991 murder of one person. Four death penalty protesters lit candles outside of the prison during his execution.

"Bohannon, Peg and Kim, I love ya'll. Son, get your life right with Christ, also your mother. Give mom a hug for me and tell her that I love her. Ya'll do understand that I came here a sinner and leaving a saint. Take me home Jesus, take me home Lord, take me home Lord. I ain't left yet, must be a miracle. I am a miracle. I see you, Rich. Don't cry son, don't cry baby. I love ya'll. I'm ready.

- Marvin Lee Wilson, executed in Texas in August 2012, for the murder of one person in 1992.



Out of these inmates, a handful chose their last statement to be about about being Black in America



There are only six of them.

"Being born black in America was against me."

- John C. Young, executed in Georgia in 1985

"They told the other two guys to put it on me. I think it's wrong. I swear to God I did not kill him." "You provided no defense. I didn't have no defense, and that's why I'm here." "My name is Luther Jerome Williams. I ain't no black, little rat."

- Luther Jerome Williams, executed in Alabama in 2007.

"...I would like to say that I did not kill Bobby Lambert. That I'm an innocent black man that is being murdered. This is a lynching that is happening in America tonight." " This is the part of the genocide, this is part of the African (unintelligible), that we as black people have endured in America. But we shall overcome, we will continue with this. We will continue, we will gain our freedom and liberation, by any means necessary. Stay strong. They cannot kill us. We will move forward."

- Gary Lee Graham, executed in Texas, in June 2000.

"This execution is not justice. This execution is an act of revenge! If this is justice, then justice is blind..."

- Richard James Wilkerson, executed in Texas in 1993 for murdering four people in 1983. His sister wrote a last statement in his name.

"I'm an African warrior, born to breathe, and born to die." -

- Carl Eugene Kelly, executed in Texas in 1993.

"I just say in this case the State of Texas made a big mistake,", "It doesn't do any good to have a lawyer. Fifty percent of the cases go before the Court of Criminal Appeals. They only hear the white ones. May the grace of God have mercy on them."

- Bernard Eugene Amos, executed in Texas for murdering one person in 1988.
